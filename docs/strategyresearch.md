#1 TRADING MISTAKES
[00:00] Summary of 10 Common Trading Mistakes and How to Fix Them

This video outlines 10 critical mistakes traders frequently make and offers practical advice on how to avoid them, aiming to significantly improve trading performance. The presenter emphasizes the importance of discipline, risk management, and structured analysis in trading.

[00:00] Mistake 1: Ignoring Market Structure

    Traders often fail to consider the bigger market structure when entering trades.
    Example: Selling during a perceived down move without recognizing the overall uptrend (higher highs and higher lows) causes stop-outs.
    Key insight: Always analyze market structure by identifying highs and lows and determining the prevailing trend before entering trades.

[01:22] Mistake 2: Not Considering High Time Frame Context

    Focusing only on lower time frames can lead to poor decisions, such as selling into strong demand zones visible on higher time frames.
    Example: A trade initiated at a supply zone fails because the higher time frame shows a significant demand zone pushing price upwards.
    Key insight: Use a top-down approach—zoom out to higher time frames to understand key support and resistance areas before trading lower time frames.

[02:18] Mistake 3: Not Using Stop-Loss Orders

    Entering trades without placing a stop-loss immediately exposes traders to unlimited risk.
    Traders sometimes delay stop placement or assume the market won’t move against them.
    Key insight: Always set a predefined stop-loss as soon as a trade is taken to protect capital from sharp adverse moves.

[03:31] Mistake 4: Chasing Price Action

    Jumping into trades after large price moves out of fear of missing out (FOMO) leads to poor entries and unfavorable risk-reward ratios.
    Price often retraces to significant levels before continuing the trend, so entering too late increases risk.
    Key insight: Be patient and wait for the market to return to high-probability zones like supply or demand areas for better entries and risk management.

[04:54] Mistake 5: Holding Trades Through Major News Events

    Economic news releases, especially high-impact ones like Non-Farm Payrolls, cause volatile spikes that can stop out even well-planned trades.
    News-driven spikes create market uncertainty as institutional players speculate on direction.
    Key insight: Avoid holding or opening trades during red-folder (high-impact) news events by consulting economic calendars (e.g., fxstreet.com).

[06:16] Mistake 6: Trading Too Large (Overleveraging)

    Using excessively large position sizes can lead to catastrophic losses or emotional panic selling during normal drawdowns.
    Large size can erode confidence and cause premature exiting of potentially profitable trades.
    Key insight: Use reasonable position sizes aligned with your account size and risk tolerance to maintain discipline and hold trades through normal fluctuations.

[07:02] Mistake 7: Risking Random Amounts on Each Trade

    Inconsistent risk per trade leads to volatile results—big wins can be wiped out by bigger losses.
    Without standardized risk control, traders face emotional swings and drawdowns.
    Key insight: Implement fixed percentage risk per trade, recommended at 1%, regardless of confidence level, to maintain controlled, sustainable growth.

[08:22] Mistake 8: Trading Through Rollover Periods

    Rollover occurs around 10:00–11:00 p.m. UK time when brokers roll the price and spreads widen dramatically.
    This can cause unexpected stop-outs without the market price touching stop levels due to spread widening.
    Key insight: Avoid trading or holding positions during the rollover hour unless trades are already significantly profitable.

[09:13] Mistake 9: Constantly Switching Strategies

    Frequently changing systems based on new tips or videos disrupts progress by resetting learning curves repeatedly.
    Lack of consistency prevents mastery and data accumulation needed for system refinement.
    Key insight: Commit to one repeatable trading system, work on refining and optimizing it to achieve exponential growth over time.

[10:28] Mistake 10: Expecting Every Trade to Win

    Winning every trade is unrealistic; top traders commonly win around 50%.
    Focus should be on risk management, risk-to-reward ratios, and win-loss ratios rather than win rate alone.

    Use stop-losses immediately to manage risk.

#3 SUPPLY DEMAND
[00:00] Introduction to Supply and Demand Fails

    Supply and demand are foundational concepts in trading, representing price zones where significant buying (demand) or selling (supply) occurs.
    These zones often predict price reactions, but sometimes they fail—meaning the price breaks through these zones instead of reversing.
    Importantly, supply and demand fails are not just setbacks; they create new trading opportunities.
    The video promises to teach how to spot such failures early and trade them effectively.

[00:25] Fundamental Theory of Supply and Demand Zones

    A demand zone is a price area where strong buying interest was previously observed, typically identified before a large bullish impulse move.
    This zone is marked by an “impulse candle” showing strong buying control (large bullish body, minimal bearish pressure).
    Conversely, a supply zone is where a significant volume of selling occurs, often before a large downward price move.
    These zones represent prices at which buyers or sellers are willing to transact, creating areas of support (demand) or resistance (supply).
    Traders expect price to react (bounce or reverse) when it returns to these zones.

[01:52] Drawing Supply and Demand Zones

    The zones are drawn around the last candle before the impulsive move:
        For demand, the last consolidation or indecision candle before a strong bullish candle.
        For supply, the last consolidation candle before a strong bearish candle.
    This method captures the exact point of major buying or selling initiation—the origin of the imbalance that drives price impulsively.

[02:43] Supply and Demand Zones Can Fail

    Sometimes, price breaches these zones instead of respecting them.
    The video presents an example where a demand zone fails as price breaks below the zone instead of bouncing.
    This failure is termed a demand fail and signals a shift in market control from buyers to sellers.
    Rather than seeing this as a loss, traders can use it to anticipate and trade the new trend direction.

[03:36] Trading Opportunities from Demand Fails

    A failed demand zone suggests the market may continue lower toward the next demand zone.
    Sellers have taken control; thus, traders can look for pullbacks to supply zones (areas of selling pressure) to enter short trades.
    The video identifies two supply zones within the example where traders could sell, aiming for the next lower demand zone as a target.
    This approach flips the initial bullish bias to a bearish one, providing a secondary trade setup.

[05:04] Summary of Trading the Demand Fail Pattern

    Initially, traders expect a reversal up from the demand zone.
    If the demand zone fails, it signals loss of buyer control and growing seller dominance.
    Traders then seek short entries from supply zones or retests, targeting lower demand zones.
    This method transforms a failed buy signal into a profitable sell strategy aligned with market momentum.

[06:04] Demand Fail Example in Practice

    The video illustrates a market attempting to buy from a demand zone after a sweep below lows but failing to confirm strength.
    Instead of breaking higher, price breaks lower, invalidating the buy idea.
    This invalidation prevents entering a losing trade and shifts focus to selling toward the next demand zone.
    Important note: Previously tested demand zones lose significance because repeated retests weaken their effectiveness.

[08:29] Identifying the Next Demand Zone and Trading Range

    After the demand fail, the market is expected to move toward the next “extreme demand” zone, which is the lowest untapped demand level.
    The price range between the broken demand and the next demand zone represents an area without strong buyer pushback.
    Traders can use this gap to enter short positions from supply zones, aiming for the next demand as a target.
    This approach treats supply and demand zones as potential flip levels that can switch roles when broken.

[10:21] Supply Fail Explained

    Supply fail is the opposite scenario: price breaks above a supply zone instead of reversing down.
    This break indicates weakening seller control and increasing buyer strength.
    The video presents an example where price breaks through supply, then moves up toward the next supply zone.
    Traders can buy from the demand zone below the failed supply, targeting the next supply zone for exits.
    This trade capitalizes on the momentum shift from sellers to buyers triggered by the supply zone failure.

[13:45] Forecasting Supply and Demand Fails Before They Occur

    The video explains that multiple retests (“taps”) of a supply or demand zone weaken the zone.
    A demand zone tapped three times shows declining buying pressure, signaling a likely break lower.
    Similarly, repeated hits on a supply zone indicate weakening sellers, suggesting a potential break higher.
    This “wearing down” metaphor likens the zone to a wall being hammered repeatedly until it breaks.
    Recognizing these signs early allows traders to position themselves ahead of the break for better entries.

[17:13] Example of Supply Zone Weakness and Flip to Buying Opportunity

    The GBP/JPY example shows a supply zone tested multiple times with weakening seller strength.
    Buyers show increasing control, evidenced by bullish candles closing near the top of the supply zone.
    This signals a probable break above supply and a flip of bias from selling to buying.
    Traders can then look for demand zones to buy from and target higher supply zones, shifting from bearish to bullish strategies.

[23:07] Conclusion

[00:24]
Market Structure Dynamics:

    In an uptrend, the market shows continuation impulse moves (upward moves breaking previous highs). These are small structural shifts where the market moves from a corrective pullback to a new impulsive leg up.
    A standard structure shift usually signifies a reversal: e.g., an uptrend moving from higher highs and higher lows to a lower low, suggesting a downtrend may start. Typically, after this shift, the market pulls back to form a lower high before continuing down.
    Similarly, in a downtrend, a structure shift to an uptrend is seen when a higher high is created, followed by a higher low, signaling continuation upwards.
    However, not every structure shift results in a reversal. Sometimes the market just performs a liquidity sweep—breaking a low or high to trigger stop orders or collect liquidity before reversing with the dominant trend.

Example: USD/JPY Market Context and Range Liquidity:

    Liquidity refers to accumulated orders in the market such as stop-losses, buy/sell stops, which create areas that price gravitates toward or reacts strongly from.
    Liquidity often clusters around equal highs/lows, trend lines, and confined ranges, termed range liquidity.
    The market is drawn to these points because large numbers of orders reside there, making these zones crucial for identifying likely reversals or continuations.

        Impulse moves: Pro-trend moves that create new highs in uptrends or new lows in downtrends. These moves determine trend direction.

Mechanizm manipulacji rynkowej opartej na handlu

    Manipulator zaczyna od kupowania akcji, stopniowo budując pozycję, unikając zwrócenia uwagi.
    Następnie używa praktyki samozawierania transakcji (self-trading): kupuje i sprzedaje akcje samemu sobie, tworząc fałszywy wolumen i wpływając na cenę rynkową.
    Gdy zyskuje kontrolę nad rynkiem, może ustalać dowolną cenę, często wyższą, i na tym zarabia.
    Ten sposób manipulacji jest kosztowny i nielegalny, ale skuteczny, zwłaszcza na rynkach o niskiej płynności i słabej regulacji.

Sposoby ukrywania manipulacji

    Rozproszenie zleceń samozakupu i samosprzedaży w czasie i cenach, aby wyglądały jak normalne działania wielu uczestników.
    Kształtowanie rozkładu cen tak, aby zakrzywiać krzywe zamówień, lecz pozostawać trudnym do wykrycia wzrokiem analityków.
    Manipulacje często współistnieją z prawdziwymi informacjami, co utrudnia rozróżnienie, czy wzrosty cen są naturalne, czy sztucznie wywołane.

Wykrywanie manipulacji

    Analiza kształtu krzywych zamówień i wolumenu w kontekście symulacji bez manipulacji pozwala ustalić zakres normalnego zachowania rynku.
    Nietypowe wzorce, takie jak duże zlecenia kupna i sprzedaży po zbliżonych cenach lub nienaturalne zmiany wolumenu, mogą wskazywać na manipulację.
    Regulacje i nadzór rynkowy są kluczowe, zwłaszcza na dużych, regulowanych rynkach, aby zapobiegać takim działaniom.

Wnioski i znaczenie

    Manipulatorzy nie muszą fałszować informacji, wystarczy, że kontrolują rynek przez handel.
    Manipulacja opiera się na przyjęciu, że cena jutra będzie bliska cenie dziś, co pozwala sztucznie zwiększać wartość aktywów.
    Rynki oparte na pełnej losowości są trudne do przewidzenia i manipulacji, ale praktyczne ograniczenia (np. majątkowe) oraz zachowania ludzi wprowadzają mechanizmy, które można wykorzystać.
    Uczciwi inwestorzy powinni być świadomi istnienia takich praktyk i zwracać uwagę na podejrzane wzorce.
    Regulacje i zaawansowane narzędzia analityczne są niezbędne, by chronić uczestników rynku przed manipulacją.

How the Simulation Works: Orders and Price Setting

    Each day, traders place buy and sell orders, some observing, some trading.
    Buyers specify a maximum price they are willing to pay for a number of shares; sellers specify a minimum price they are willing to accept.
    Both buy and sell prices are modeled as probability distributions (bell curves) around the previous day’s market price, representing traders’ expectations.
    Orders are random within these distributions, simulating a fair and unpredictable market.

[03:37]
Auction Pricing Explained

    The market price for the day is set by maximizing total satisfaction, i.e., the overlap where buy prices meet sell prices to maximize traded volume.
    The price range where buy and sell orders overlap determines the day’s price and traded volume.
    A single price is chosen in this overlap (often the midpoint) to maximize agreement between buyers and sellers.
    This is called auction pricing, commonly used at market openings in real-world markets.
    The simulation assumes one trading moment per day with auction pricing.

[05:18]
Simulation Loop and Market Evolution

    Buyers and sellers share identical bell-curve order price distributions centered on yesterday’s price, reflecting stable expectations.
    The market price and volume for the day are computed, then used as the base for the next day’s distributions, creating a random walk of prices over time.
    The market thus moves randomly, making future price directions unpredictable.

[06:22]
Incorporating External Events and Expectations

    Real markets are influenced by external information like news, shifting traders’ expectations.
    Good news raises perceived company value, causing prices to jump as traders anticipate future gains.
    The simulation simplifies this by letting traders instantly adjust expectations to new price levels upon news arrival.
    Expectations about future news also factor into current prices, causing near-instant price adjustments.
    However, in this simulation, traders ignore all news and rumors, acting purely randomly, ensuring the market is as hard to manipulate as possible.

    Large price jumps can result from low volume rather than news, important for interpreting market movements.

    Example: placing a large buy order at a higher price raises the market price by increasing demand.

    This tactic distorts price and volume signals, tricking traders into poor decisions.
    It is easier in smaller, less regulated markets due to lower costs.
